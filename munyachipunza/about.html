The name

I was born in Zimbabwe and given the name Munyaradzi — which means one who comforts, one who gives hope. It's a heavy name to carry. For most of my life, I wasn't sure I deserved it.

Names in Shona culture aren't decorative. They're declarations. A name like mine comes with an expectation — that the person who bears it will be, in some meaningful way, a source of something for the people around them. I spent years trying to figure out what that looked like for me.

"A name like mine comes with an expectation. I spent years trying to figure out what that looked like."

The hard seasons

I've sat with hard seasons. I've buried my mother. I've carried an ageing father while trying to hold a family together across two countries. I've led teams under real pressure, made decisions with incomplete information, and managed the particular loneliness of being the person everyone else depends on.

I know what it is to look capable on the outside and feel uncertain on the inside. I know what it's like to carry more than you let on. To pray in the dark and hear nothing back. To keep going anyway — not because you're strong, but because the people around you need you to be.

Why I write

Not because I've figured it out. Not because I'm on the other side of anything. But because I've found that when someone else names what you're feeling — when they say me too in a way that actually reaches you — something shifts. You feel less alone. And feeling less alone is sometimes the first step toward finding your footing again.

That's what I'm trying to do here. One reflection at a time. Short pieces, honest and human, for anyone who's in a season that's harder than they expected.

"When someone names what you're feeling — something shifts. You feel less alone."

Who I am today

I'm a Zimbabwean living and working in Cape Town. By day, I lead operations for a manufacturing company — 30 people, real stakes, the kind of work that keeps you honest about what leadership actually requires. I'm a father to two sons who remind me daily what matters most. A runner. A reader. A man of imperfect, persistent faith.

I write about all of it — the doubts and the convictions, the setbacks and the slow recoveries, the moments when faith felt distant and the moments it held. Not as a polished authority. As a fellow traveller.

If you're in a hard season right now — welcome. You're in the right place.

The book

A book for the journey

For three years I wrote a book. It's about biblical manhood — not performance, not perfection, but the slow, honest work of becoming who God called you to be. Twelve chapters. A study guide. Written in the margins of a full life.

It was written for men who are trying. Who are carrying things. Who believe — or want to — but are tired of being told what that should look like from the outside.
